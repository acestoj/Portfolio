Sheet 00 — About

What I work on

I'm a third-year Mechanical Engineering (Honours) student at Deakin University, Burwood — part of the campus's first engineering cohort. My work sits at the intersection of structural analysis, finite element validation and real client problems: chain hoists that jam mid-show, footings that hit unexpected ground conditions, a motorsport program that needs a credible relaunch.

I like the moment a stress contour turns from red to blue just as much as the moment a stakeholder says yes.

Outside of FEA, I marshal motorsport circuits at Preston Motorcycle Club and chase early snow at Mt Buller — a different kind of risk assessment, same attention to the edge cases.

Sheet 01 / 04 Client: Melbourne Park Year: 2026 Tools: ABAQUS · Fusion 360

SpiderGuide — Universal Chain Guide Attachment

Objective: design a universal chain guide attachment for Melbourne Park's electric chain hoists, fixing a chain-spooling fault in their live-rigging system — no tools to install, no modification to the hoist, under 5 kg.

I benchmarked five products already on the market, converted the client's verbal requirements into hard design criteria, and led the structural validation of the final assembly — an ABAQUS model covering static, frequency and buckling cases across every AL6061 component.

Sheet 02 / 04 Role: Co-Lead Status: In progress 2026 – Present

Establishing Formula SAE at Deakin Burwood

Objective: build a credible, self-sufficient case for Deakin Burwood's first Formula SAE program — Geelong already runs one, Burwood doesn't, and the leadership team has reason to be cautious after past student projects overran their budgets.

I'm co-leading the proposal: working directly with the existing program's technical lead, scoping a structure the team can run themselves, and building a staged plan with realistic first-two-year milestones rather than promising a race car on day one.

Result (in progress): concept and scope are locked in; stakeholder alignment across both campuses and the leadership business case are both underway.

Sheet 03 / 04 Client: City of Stonnington Year: 2025 Tools: SPACE GASS

Cyclist & Pedestrian Bridge — City of Stonnington

Objective: develop the preliminary structural design for a cable-stayed cyclist and pedestrian bridge for the City of Stonnington — main girder, A-frame tower, stay cables and footing — and verify it independently rather than trusting the software alone.

I modelled the full system in SPACE GASS, cross-checked the output against independent hand calculations, and adapted when the brief changed: a revised geotechnical report dropped the allowable bearing pressure to 100 kPa partway through. Rather than restart the structural system, I redesigned the footing alone and revalidated it against the same stability requirements.

Sheet 04 / 04 Competition: Weir Warman "Project FAMINE" Year: 2024 Tools: Arduino · CAD

Warman Design & Build Competition — Seed Collection & Deposit Machine

Objective: design and build an autonomous mechanism — inside a 400 mm cube, single-activation, 120-second window — to collect and deposit seed pods (tennis balls) onto a pre-built mobile platform, as part of SEM200 Machine Design and Deakin's entry to the 2024 Weir Warman "Project FAMINE" challenge.

As a team, we built a collapsible, rotating arm controlled via Arduino, run through a full product-development cycle: problem formulation, concept generation and scored concept screening, safety design and FMEA, prototyping, CAD, and a final presentation. My contribution was the drivetrain connection — a cog linking to the steel rod that drives the platform's wheels — and the arm's servo logic, which had to recognise its own position and ascend or descend to pick seeds at different heights, all inside the 120-second limit.

Result: the mechanism worked — the drivetrain held up and the arm reliably found its pickup positions — but motor reliability issues surfaced late in the build, and the team ran out of semester time to resolve them before locking in a sub-120-second run. The right call given the time box, but the clearest lesson from the project: surface integration issues earlier, even at the cost of a less-polished individual component.
